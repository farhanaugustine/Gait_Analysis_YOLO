Spinal CPG network: the coordination layer

Each limb has its own rhythmic drive, but coupling between limbs is what keeps gait stable. This panel makes the math intuitive instead of overwhelming.

dθi/dt = ωi + Σ Kij sin(θj − θi) + ξi
where ω = intrinsic rhythm and K = coordination strength.
Four limb oscillators are tightly coupled.
At low exposure, coupling remains strong and limb rhythms lock into a coordinated trot. With stronger exposure, K weakens and noise increases, so the links look fragmented and the rhythm dials wobble.
Plain-language interpretation
ω (intrinsic rhythm): stable K (coupling strength): strong

Solvent vapor

Solvent effects are shown as a bloodstream-dominant route because inhaled vapors rapidly enter the body and can produce central nervous system effects. Toluene is a common illustrative example.

  • ATSDR notes inhalation is the most common route for toluene exposure and lists CNS findings including dizziness, tremor, and ataxia.
  • NIOSH reports acute solvent exposure can impair coordination and body balance, with chronic exposure linked to neurobehavioral changes.

Metal particulates

Ultrafine or insoluble particulate metals are shown favoring the olfactory route because animal studies and reviews support nose-to-brain transport through olfactory pathways.

  • A mouse iron-soot study directly visualized transport from the nose toward the olfactory bulb.
  • A recent review summarizes evidence that inhaled nanoparticles can enter the olfactory bulb via olfactory neurons and, in some cases, the trigeminal system.
Iron-soot olfactory transport study
2025 review on particulate pathways to the brain

Carbon monoxide

CO is shown as a systemic exposure because it causes hypoxic injury after uptake through the lungs. The basal ganglia emphasis reflects classic imaging findings in CO poisoning.

  • MR reviews describe frequent globus pallidus injury and other basal ganglia involvement after CO poisoning.
  • Chronic sequelae can include parkinsonism and other motor impairments, which is why the gait panel becomes more hesitant and unstable.
AJNR review on CO brain injury
2024 case review of neurologic sequelae after CO poisoning

Nicotine / cigarette smoke

Nicotine is shown as a milder, predominantly systemic motor-circuit exposure. The visual emphasis is on basal ganglia and cerebellar modulation, with more subtle gait variability than the harsher toxicants above.

  • Human imaging reviews describe nicotine and tobacco effects in cortico-basal ganglia-thalamic circuitry relevant to motor control.
  • Motor-task imaging and balance studies suggest nicotine can alter cerebellar recruitment, postural stability, and gait-related timing rather than producing one single severe gait signature.
